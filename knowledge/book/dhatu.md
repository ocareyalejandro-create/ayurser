# What We Are Made Of — The Tissues (Dhātu)

texts (public domain), informed by the teachers we credit in the References. We
learned from them; we did not copy them.*

## What they are

We have spoken of the forces that move the body and the fire that feeds it. Now
the body itself: what it is actually built from, and how it builds.

Ayurveda counts seven tissues — *dhātus* — and the word means *that which holds
together*. They are the physical substance of a person, layered from the most
fluid to the most refined:

1. **Rasa** — plasma, lymph, the nourishing fluid
2. **Rakta** — blood
3. **Māṃsa** — muscle
4. **Meda** — fat
5. **Asthi** — bone
6. **Majjā** — marrow and nerve tissue
7. **Śukra / ārtava** — the reproductive essence

The order is not a list. It is a *sequence of making* — and that is the whole
teaching.

## How the body builds itself

Here is the idea that changes how you eat and how you heal. Each tissue is formed
from the one before it. The food you digest first becomes **rasa**, the
nourishing fluid; a portion of that rasa is in turn "cooked" — by its own tissue
fire, the *dhātu-agni* — into **rakta**, blood; a portion of the blood becomes
**muscle**; muscle feeds **fat**; fat feeds **bone**; bone feeds **marrow and
nerve**; and the last, finest distillation becomes **śukra**, the reproductive
essence — and beyond even that, *ojas*, the subtle sap of vitality (its own
chapter follows).

Nourishment, then, does not arrive everywhere at once. It seeps *inward*, layer
by layer, each tissue taking its share and passing the refined remainder on. The
classical texts give this passage a timescale — that to nourish all seven tissues
fully, from the first meal to the deepest tissue, takes the better part of a
month.

Sit with what that means. **You cannot rebuild a depleted body in a day.** Deep
tissue — bone, nerve, reproductive vitality — is fed last and slowly, by a long
chain of good digestion repeated day after day. This is why Ayurveda is patient,
and why it distrusts the quick fix: the body is built the way a tree is built,
ring by ring, and there is no hurrying the rings.

## The seven, and how each speaks

Each tissue has a work, a sign of strength, and a sign of want — and you can
often read them on yourself.

- **Rasa (plasma).** The first nourishment, the body's moisture and contentment.
  Strong: skin with a glow, an easy fullness, calm. Depleted: dryness, fatigue, a
  thin restlessness, a vague dissatisfaction — the tissue of contentment running
  low.
- **Rakta (blood).** Vitality, warmth, colour; close kin to the fire. Strong:
  good colour, lustre, courage. Disturbed: pallor and weakness when low; heat,
  rashes, inflammation when in excess.
- **Māṃsa (muscle).** Strength and the body's covering — what protects and gives
  form. Strong: stamina, steadiness, a body that holds its shape. Low: wasting,
  weakness, a sense of being unprotected.
- **Meda (fat).** Lubrication, insulation, and — the texts are tender here — the
  tissue of love and affection. Strong: smooth joints, warmth, an easy
  fondness. In excess (the earth's tissue overgrowing): heaviness, sluggishness,
  the damp weight of too much.
- **Asthi (bone).** Structure and support, the body's frame. It is governed,
  interestingly, by the *wind* — for bone is porous and dry, and it is the wind's
  dryness that wears it. Strong: sound bones, teeth, nails. Disturbed: brittle
  bones, aching joints, dry cracking nails and hair.
- **Majjā (marrow and nerve).** What fills the bones and runs the nerves — depth,
  sensation, the substance of feeling. Strong: deep sleep, steady sensation, a
  filled-in fullness. Low: emptiness, a hollow tiredness, dryness of the deep.
- **Śukra / ārtava (reproductive essence).** The last and finest tissue, distilled
  from all the rest — fertility, vigour, and the body's overflow of vitality.
  When the whole chain is well fed, this tissue brims, and its surplus becomes
  *ojas*. When the body is depleted or driven too hard, it is the first deep
  reserve to be spent.

## How it goes wrong — the cascade

Because the tissues are a chain, their troubles are usually a chain too. Weaken
the fire and the very first tissue, rasa, is poorly made — and every tissue
*downstream* is then fed on thin nourishment. A long-undernourished person is not
missing one thing; they are missing the end of a chain that failed at the
beginning. This is why Ayurveda so often starts not with the deep complaint but
with the fire and the first nourishment: mend the source, and the river fills
again, in order, in time.

The opposite trouble is excess — most familiarly in *meda*, fat, the earth's
tissue, overgrowing into heaviness. Here the work is the reverse: lighten, kindle
the fire, and let the overfull tissue come back to measure.

## In a breath

You are built from what you digest, inward and slowly, one tissue feeding the
next — moisture to blood to muscle to fat to bone to marrow to the deep essence,
and a month for the nourishment to reach the bottom. Mend the fire and the first
tissue, be patient, and the body rebuilds itself ring by ring. There is no
hurrying it, and there is no need to.

## The classical voice

The texts describe the tissues not as parts to be catalogued but as a living
order of transformation — food becoming flesh becoming the subtle essence of
vitality, each step a small fire refining what the last one made. To care for the
body, in this view, is simply to tend that long refinement well. *(After the
Caraka Saṃhitā and Suśruta Saṃhitā, Sūtrasthāna, on the dhātus — rendered in our
own words.)*

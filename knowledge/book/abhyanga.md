# Oil — Abhyanga

*Part III, chapter four. Authored in our own voice, grounded in the classical
texts (public domain), informed by the teachers we credit in the References. We
learned from them; we did not copy them.*

---

## What it is

*Abhyanga* is the daily anointing of the body with warm oil — a few quiet minutes
of oiling the skin before the bath. It is the heart of the morning routine, and
for the dry, cold, ageing quality of the wind there is no closer answer. But it
is more than a remedy. It is, plainly, a small daily act of being cared for — by
your own hands.

## What the texts promise it

The classical praise of daily oiling is generous, and worth knowing because it
sets the stakes. The *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya* holds that oiling the body each day wards
off age and fatigue, settles the wind, builds firmness and softness together,
strengthens the body against strain, steadies sleep, and keeps the skin and eyes
clear. As an old strap stays supple with oil and a worn axle runs longer for it,
so the body, oiled daily, wears well. *(After the Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya, Sūtrasthāna
2.8–9 — rendered in our own words.)*

## How it is done

There is no technique to master, only a little care:

- **Warm the oil** — stand the bottle in hot water until it is comfortably warm.
- **Take your time** — long strokes along the long bones, circles at the joints;
  toward the body's centre is the common counsel.
- **Don't rush the edges** — the **soles of the feet** and the **crown of the
  head** first of all, then the ears. These are where the oiling does its
  quietest, deepest work, and where a restless mind is most easily settled.
- **Let it sit** — a few minutes, while it sinks in. Then a warm (not hot) bath.

## The oil for the weather

- **Wind** — warm **sesame** oil: heavy, warming, the exact opposite of the
  wind's dry cold.
- **Fire** — a cooling oil, **sunflower** or **coconut**, used cooler, not hot.
  (Coconut is common in modern practice; the classical cooling choices are
  sunflower and sandalwood — worth confirming with a practitioner.)
- **Earth** — little oil, or none. Earth does better with a **dry rub**
  (*udvartana*) — a brisk rub that lightens and rouses where oil would weigh.

## When to skip it

The texts are clear that oiling is not always the answer. Set it aside when the
earth or the undigested residue is high — heaviness, congestion, indigestion,
fever — and just after eating. When in doubt, the dry rub or simply nothing.

## Why it belongs here

This app is, at heart, a way to keep a practice alive in an ordinary life. Of all
the practices, this is the gentlest to keep and the kindest to feel: five minutes,
warm oil, your own two hands, before the day takes you. It asks almost nothing and
gives back warmth, steadiness, and the simple sense of having tended yourself.

## In a breath

Warm oil, a few unhurried minutes, the feet and the crown first. Daily, it settles
the wind, eases the body, and steadies the mind — and it is, quietly, a way of
being kind to yourself every single morning.

# What Ayurveda Is

*The doorway in. Authored in our own voice; the sources and the method are set
out in the Author's Note that opens the book.*

---

Before any of its parts — before the forces and the fire, the tastes and the
tissues — it helps to know what kind of thing Ayurveda is. Because it is not
quite what a modern reader expects of a medicine.

## The knowledge of living

The word itself is plain: *āyus*, life; *veda*, knowledge. The knowledge of life
— or more simply, the art of living well. It is among the oldest of the living
medical traditions, still practised today much as it was set down two thousand
years ago, and it began not in a laboratory but in long, patient *attention* —
generations of people watching how the body answers to food, to weather, to
rest, to the turning of the seasons, and writing down what held true.

So Ayurveda is not a doctrine to believe. It is a way of paying attention. You do
not have to accept anything on faith; you are only invited to notice your own
body more closely, and given a language for what you find.

## Health is balance — and balance moves

If there is a single idea beneath everything, it is this: **health is balance,
and balance is not a place you arrive but a thing you keep.**

The body is always being pulled off-centre — by a cold wind, a hard week, a heavy
meal, a season turning — and health is the constant, quiet work of coming back.
Not rigidity, but adjustment: a sailor trimming to the wind, not a statue holding
still. Illness, in this view, is simply balance lost and not yet recovered; and
healing is less about fighting a disease than about restoring the conditions in
which health returns on its own. *Tend the ground, and the garden grows.*

## Living in tune with nature

The reason the same wisdom keeps working is that we are not separate from the
world. The forces that move the weather — wind, heat, damp, cold, heaviness —
move in us too. The day has its rhythm and so do we; the year has its seasons and
so do we. To live well, in this tradition, is to live *with* those rhythms rather
than against them: to warm what is cold, to lighten what is heavy, to slow when
the world says slow.

## It treats the person, not the complaint

One more thing sets Ayurveda apart, and it matters for everything that follows:
it treats the *person*, not the illness. Two people with the same complaint may
need opposite things, because they are different people, in different weather, on
different days. There is no single right breakfast, no remedy that suits
everyone. There is only *you*, today — and the art of reading that, and answering
it kindly.

## How to read this book

What follows is the language and the practice, in order. First the world we are
made of — the elements, the qualities you can feel, the tastes you can eat. Then
the three forces those make in us. Then the fire that digests, the rhythms of the
day and year, the body's deeper make-up, and the mind. You can read it straight
through, or open it where your own life is asking a question. Take what serves
and leave the rest; it will keep.

One honest word before we begin: this is wisdom for living — for food, rest,
rhythm, and ease — and not a substitute for medicine. It will give you a language
for what you already feel and gentle ways back to balance. For anything that
needs a healer, it will send you to one. (More on that promise at the close.)

That is the whole of it, really: *notice how you are, understand why, and gently
find your way back.* Everything else is detail.

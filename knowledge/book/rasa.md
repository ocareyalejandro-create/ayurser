## What they are

Taste is the first thing food tells the body. Before a single nutrient is
counted, the tongue has already read the meal and begun to answer it. Ayurveda
takes this seriously: *rasa*, taste, is not pleasure alone but information and
medicine, and it counts **six** — each born of the elements, each moving the
three forces in its own direction.

To eat well is not to chase one taste or banish another. It is to know what each
one does, and to lean on the ones your weather needs.

## The six

**Sweet** *(earth + water)* — building, grounding, cooling. The taste of grains,
milk, ghee, sweet ripe fruit, most cooked vegetables. It nourishes and steadies;
it settles the wind and the fire. In excess it adds to the earth — heaviness,
dullness, weight.

**Sour** *(earth + fire)* — warming, sharpening the appetite, moistening. Citrus,
yoghurt, fermented things, tamarind. It kindles digestion and grounds the wind;
too much stokes the fire and the earth.

**Salty** *(water + fire)* — warming, moistening, softening, stimulating. Salt,
seaweed. A little wakes digestion and settles the wind; too much heats the fire
and holds water in the earth.

**Pungent** *(fire + air)* — heating, drying, lightening; the great kindler of a
slow fire. Chilli, ginger, black pepper, garlic, onion. It lifts the earth's
heaviness; in excess it inflames the fire and dries out the wind.

**Bitter** *(air + ether)* — cooling, drying, lightening, clearing. Leafy greens,
turmeric, bitter melon, many herbs. It cools the fire and lightens the earth;
too much, being so dry and light, provokes the wind.

**Astringent** *(air + earth)* — cooling, drying, drawing-together. Beans,
pomegranate, unripe banana, green tea. It cools the fire and tightens the
earth's damp; in excess it, too, unsettles the wind.

## How they move the three forces

The whole of it folds into three plain rules:

- **The wind** is settled by the **sweet, sour and salty** (warm, moist,
  grounding) — and stirred up by the pungent, bitter and astringent (dry,
  light).
- **The fire** is cooled by the **sweet, bitter and astringent** — and inflamed
  by the pungent, sour and salty (hot).
- **The earth** is lightened by the **pungent, bitter and astringent** (light,
  dry, hot) — and weighed down by the sweet, sour and salty (heavy, moist).

You can feel the symmetry: what grounds the wind tends to feed the earth; what
lightens the earth tends to unsettle the wind. The art is balance, read against
today.

## The balanced plate

A whole meal touches all six tastes — that is the old measure of a complete
plate, and the reason a good thali holds something sweet, something sour,
something pungent, something bitter, and so on. When your weather is even, eat
all six. When one force is speaking, lean on the tastes that quiet it, and go
gently on those that feed it. The tongue, tended this way, becomes a daily guide.

## Today, try this

At your next meal, look for the missing taste. If the plate is all sweet and
heavy, add something bitter or pungent — a few greens, a little ginger. A plate
that holds all six tastes is a plate that holds you closer to balance.

## In a breath

Six tastes, six tools. Sweet, sour and salt to ground; pungent, bitter and
astringent to lighten and cool. Read your weather, and reach for the ones it asks
for.

## The classical voice

The texts trace each taste back to the five elements and forward to its effect
on the three forces — taste as the bridge between what the world is made of and
what it does inside us. *(After the Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya, Sūtrasthāna, on rasa —
rendered in our own words.)*

# The Senses — the doors we guard

*Authored in our own voice, grounded in the classical texts (public domain),
informed by the teachers we credit in the References. We learned from them; we
did not copy them.*

---

## What they are

The senses are the doors between the world and the mind. Everything that reaches
us — every sight, sound, taste, touch, and smell — comes through them, and what
comes through shapes the mind as surely as food shapes the body. Ayurveda takes
this with full seriousness: the eyes and the ears have a *diet*, and it can
nourish or it can poison.

There is a neat correspondence, each sense tied to an element: **hearing** to
space, **touch** to air, **sight** to fire, **taste** to water, **smell** to
earth — the five elements meeting us through five gates.

## You are what you take in

We have said it of food: you are not what you eat but what you digest. It is just
as true of impressions. A mind fed all day on noise, hurry, alarm, and an endless
scroll of bright fragments is a mind being fed junk — and it shows, as
restlessness, agitation, a fog that won't lift. A mind fed on quiet, on beauty, on
the natural world, on the faces of people who steady you, is a mind well
nourished, and it shows as ease.

Most of us guard the gut more carefully than the senses, though the senses pour
in far more, far faster. Ayurveda would have us tend both.

## The right use of the senses

The classical counsel is simple and threefold. Disturbance comes from using the
senses **too much**, **too little**, or **wrongly**:

- **Too much** — sensory overload: the over-bright, over-loud, over-stimulated
  day that leaves the mind frayed.
- **Too little** — starvation: too little beauty, too little nature, too little
  real human contact.
- **Wrongly** — the wrong impressions: a steady diet of what agitates,
  coarsens, or frightens.

Balance here is the same art as everywhere else: not too much, not too little,
the right things, at the right time. Especially at the edges of the day — what you
let in first thing in the morning and last thing at night sets the mind's weather
for the hours on either side.

## Guarding the doors

So this is a practice, quiet and entirely within reach: *curate what you let in.*
Begin the day before the screen, not on it. Give the eyes the sky and the ears
some silence. Take in something beautiful on purpose. Step away when the input
turns to noise. None of this is new-age advice — it is among the oldest
prescriptions in the tradition, and the modern instinct toward a "digital detox"
is simply Ayurveda's care of the senses, rediscovered.

It is also, quietly, the principle this app is built on. A tool meant to steady
you should not farm your attention; it should give you one calm thing and send
you back to your life. An app that guards your senses rather than raiding them is
the only kind worth keeping on your morning.

## Today, try this

Guard one edge of the day: keep the first ten minutes after waking, *or* the last
ten before sleep, free of the screen. Give the senses that much quiet, and watch
the mind follow.

## In a breath

The senses are doors, and what comes through them feeds the mind. Tend that diet
as you tend your food: not too much, not too little, and chosen with care —
especially at the day's two edges. Guard the doors, and the mind grows quiet of
its own accord.

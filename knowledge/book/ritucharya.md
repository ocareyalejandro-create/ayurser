## What it is

As the day turns through the three forces, so does the year. *Ritucharya* — the
seasonal routine — is the simple art of living *with* the season rather than
against it: eating and resting in a way that answers the weather outside, because
the weather outside becomes the weather within.

The pattern is steady across the traditions: each season first lets a force
quietly **accumulate**, then, as the weather shifts, **provokes** it. Knowing
this, you can meet a force before it speaks.

## The year's three weathers

- **Late winter into spring — the earth's season.** Cold and damp gather the
  earth through winter; the spring thaw melts it loose, and it floods (the heavy
  limbs, the congestion, the spring cold). Meet it by *lightening*: warm, dry,
  well-spiced food; less oil, sugar and dairy; more movement; the impulse to
  spring-clean is the body's own.

- **Summer into early autumn — the fire's season.** Heat builds the fire through
  the long bright months. Meet it by *cooling*: sweet, bitter and astringent
  food, plenty of water, ease over effort, shade in the middle hours. Don't add
  fire to fire.

- **Autumn into early winter — the wind's season.** Cold, dry, moving air lifts
  the wind (the dry skin, the restlessness, the broken sleep). Meet it by
  *grounding*: warm, oily, cooked, nourishing food; oil on the body; warmth; and,
  above all, routine.

## The turn between

The classical texts give a particular warning about the **junctions** between
seasons — the week or two when one weather gives way to the next. This is when we
most often fall ill, because the body is still dressed for the season that's
leaving. The counsel is gentleness: change your food and habits *gradually*
across the turn, never all at once. Ease out of one season and into the next.

## Eat what the season grows

There is a plainer wisdom folded inside all of this, and the traditions and good
sense agree on it: the season tends to grow what the season needs. Cooling juicy
fruits arrive in the heat; dense grounding roots keep through the cold. To eat
local and in season is, more often than not, to eat the very thing the weather is
asking for.

## Today, try this

Ask what season it is outside your window, and meet it just once today: something
light and pungent if it's damp spring, something cool and sweet if it's high
summer, something warm and oily if it's dry autumn.

## In a breath

Live with the year, not against it. Lighten in spring, cool in summer, ground in
autumn — and ease gently through the turns between. The body that moves with the
season rarely has to fight it.

## The classical voice

The texts lay out the year season by season, each with its foods and habits, as
a single unbroken teaching: health is not a fixed regimen but a conversation with
the changing world. *(After the classical ritucharya of the Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya and
Caraka Saṃhitā — rendered in our own words.)*

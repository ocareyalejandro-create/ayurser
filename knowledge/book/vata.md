# Vāta — the Wind

*The first chapter of the book we are writing. Authored in our own voice and
structure, grounded in the classical texts (quoted only from public-domain
translations), and informed by the modern teachers we credit in the References.
We learned from them; we did not copy them.*

---

## What it is

Of the three forces, Vāta is the first, because nothing moves without it. Vāta
is the principle of movement itself — every motion in the body is its work: the
breath drawn in and let go, the blood on its rounds, a thought crossing the
mind, food carried downward, the body's wastes shown the door.

The classical texts give it six qualities, and to know them is to know Vāta
entirely: it is **dry, cold, light, mobile, subtle, and rough** (*Aṣṭāṅga
Hṛdaya*, Sūtrasthāna 1). Wherever these gather in a life, the wind gathers with
them. It is named the leader of the three — Pitta and Kapha, the texts say, are
lame without it, carried like clouds before a wind. So when the wind is steady,
the whole system is steady; and when it is disturbed, it is the first thing
disturbed, and it disturbs the rest.

## How it feels when it rises

You do not need the word *Vāta* to know its weather. You have lived it:

- a mind that will not settle — racing, scattered, anxious; many doors open and
  none walked through
- sleep gone light and broken, waking at three with the wheels already turning
- the body cold at its edges, hands and feet, and dry — skin, lips, the cracked
  heel
- digestion turned irregular: hungry then not, bloated, gassy, dry
- a restlessness, a difficulty landing; speaking quickly, moving quickly, doing
  much
- and beneath it, a thin tiredness, because the wind spends what it does not have

This is autumn weather, in a person. It is the traveller's state, the
overworked state, the too-much-screen-and-too-little-warmth state.

## Why it tips

*Like increases like* — this is the whole law (*Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*, Sūtrasthāna
1.13½). Because the wind is dry, cold, light and mobile, the dry, the cold, the
light and the moving feed it:

- food that is raw, cold, dry, or taken in a hurry; skipped meals; too little oil
- cold air, cold wind, the turn into autumn
- travel, irregular hours, too little sleep
- too much talk, thought, screen-light, stimulation; movement without rest
- worry, fear, grief
- and age itself — the later years are the wind's season of life

## How it is met

*Opposites restore balance.* Against dry, oil. Against cold, warmth. Against the
light and the mobile, the heavy and the steady. The wind is never disciplined
into stillness — it is *gentled* into it: coaxed, warmed, oiled, given walls.

**At the table.** Warm, cooked, moist, lightly oiled food — soups, stews, cooked
grains, stewed fruit, warm milk, a little ghee. The sweet, sour and salty tastes
settle the wind; the pungent, bitter and astringent — the dry, light tastes —
provoke it (*Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*, Sūtrasthāna 1). Not the cold raw salad on a windy
day. And the manner matters as much as the meal: eaten warm, eaten slowly, eaten
at the same hours. The wind feeds on irregularity, so a steady table is itself
medicine.

**The morning.** Because the wind is irregular, a predictable morning pacifies
it before anything else does. The classical sequence is plain: rise before the
sun, scrape the tongue, warm water — and, the heart of it for Vāta, *abhyanga*,
the oiling of the body (*Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*, Sūtrasthāna 2). Warm sesame oil worked
into the skin, and above all into the soles of the feet and the crown of the
head. The texts hold daily oiling as the thing that withstands age and
exertion; for the dry, cold, ageing quality of the wind there is no closer
answer. Same anchors, same hours — give the wind its walls.

**The breath.** Slow, even, unhurried — never fast or forceful while the wind is
high. The gentle alternate-nostril breath (*Nāḍī Śodhana*) steadies a scattered
morning.

**The body in motion.** Steadiness over intensity: grounding shapes, an
unhurried walk, slow forward folds — not the jarring, the fast, or the
depleting. The wind asks to be settled, not spent.

**The season and the hour.** Vāta rises in autumn, in cold dry wind, and in the
last dark hours before dawn. Meet those times with more warmth, more oil, more
rest — not less.

## The classical voice

The *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya* sets the whole of it down in a single line, and we keep
returning to it because it is the engine beneath everything above:

> "Like increases like; opposites lead to decrease."
> — *Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*, Sūtrasthāna 1.13½

All we have said about the wind is only this law, applied with care.

## In a breath

The wind is dryness, cold, and motion. Meet it with oil, warmth, and steadiness
— gently, and daily — and it remembers how to be still.

# Sleep — Nidrā

*Part III, chapter three. Authored in our own voice, grounded in the classical
texts (public domain), informed by the teachers we credit in the References. We
learned from them; we did not copy them.*

---

## What it is

Ayurveda counts sleep among the three pillars on which a life stands — with food,
and with the wise use of one's energy. Not a luxury, not lost time, but one of
the three supports holding the whole body up. The texts are direct about it:
happiness and misery, strength and weakness, clarity and confusion, even the
length of life — all turn, in part, on how a person sleeps.

Right sleep, at the right time, restores. Too little, too much, or sleep taken at
the wrong hour quietly undoes.

## The right hours

Sleep keeps the same clock as the rest of the day. The deepest, most restoring
rest falls in the early-night **earth hours**, when the body is heavy and ready
to be still. So the old counsel is to be asleep *before* the night **fire hour**
around ten — past it, the fire lends a second wind and the mind comes back to
life. And to wake in the pre-dawn **wind hour**, when the air is light and the
head is clearest.

A simple shape: down before the night turns, up before the sun.

## Sleep, by the three forces

- **Wind** sleeps lightly and broken — falling asleep late, waking at three with
  the mind turning. It is helped by warmth and weight: a warm bath or warm milk,
  oil on the feet, a quiet unhurried evening, the same bedtime each night.
- **Fire** sleeps well enough but wakes hot in the small hours, often arguing
  with something. It is helped by cool and calm: a cool room, no late work, no
  late screens, nothing that stokes the heat before bed.
- **Earth** sleeps long and deep and still wants more, waking heavy. It is helped
  by *less* — an earlier rise, no daytime naps, and movement to shake off the
  weight.

## A word on daytime sleep

The texts generally caution against sleeping in the day: it adds to the earth and
to the undigested residue, *āma*. They allow it in summer's heat, and for the
genuinely depleted, the ill, the very young and very old — but for most, on most
days, the day is for waking and the night is for sleep.

## The evening that earns it

Good sleep is not seized at bedtime; it is prepared all evening. A light dinner,
early. Dimming light. Slowing down. Warmth — oil worked into the soles of the
feet is the oldest answer for a restless mind. And the screen set aside well
before the pillow.

## Today, try this

Tonight, set the screen down half an hour before bed and warm your feet — socks,
or a little oil. Let the evening lean toward rest, and let sleep come to meet you.

## In a breath

Sleep is one of the three things holding your life up. Be down before the night
turns, up before the sun, and let the whole evening lean gently toward rest. Tend
your sleep and it tends everything else.

## The classical voice

The texts name sleep a pillar of life alongside food and the careful spending of
one's energy — three supports, and the body falters if any gives way. *(After the
Caraka Saṃhitā, Sūtrasthāna, on the three upholders of life — rendered in our own
words.)*

# Pitta — the Fire

*Authored in our own voice, grounded in the classical texts
(public domain), informed by the teachers we credit in the References. We
learned from them; we did not copy them.*

---

## What it is

If Vāta is movement, Pitta is transformation. It is the fire of the body — the
heat that cooks food into flesh, light into sight, experience into
understanding. Every change that turns one thing into another is its work:
digestion, warmth, colour, courage, and the sharp clear edge of the intellect.

The classical texts give it its qualities: **hot, sharp, light, spreading, and
faintly oily**, with a sour, pungent edge (*Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*, Sūtrasthāna 1).
Where heat and sharpness gather, the fire gathers. Balanced, it gives a bright
complexion, strong digestion, and a keen mind; provoked, it turns and consumes
its own house.

## How it feels when it rises

You have felt the fire run high:

- a temper close to the surface — irritable, critical, impatient, sharp with
  those you love
- heat in the body: flushed skin, inflammation, acidity, heartburn, loose and
  burning stools
- a fierce hunger, and anger when a meal is late — the fire turning on itself
- skin that breaks out, reddens, burns
- waking in the small hours, hot, the mind already arguing
- a focus so sharp it tips into striving, competition, never enough

This is high-summer weather, in a person. It is the driven state, the deadline
state, the hungry-and-furious state.

## Why it tips

*Like increases like.* Because the fire is hot, sharp and sour, the hot, the
sharp and the sour feed it:

- spicy, sour, salty, fermented and fried food; alcohol, coffee
- the midday sun, the heat of summer
- skipping meals — a hungry fire burns its own walls
- deadlines, competition, anger, criticism, the will to win
- and the middle years — midlife is the fire's season

## How it is met

*Opposites restore balance.* Against heat, coolness. Against the sharp, the
soft. Against the sour and pungent, the sweet, the bitter, the astringent. The
fire is not fought — it is *cooled*, and given room.

**At the table.** Cooling, sweet and bitter food — sweet ripe fruit, leafy
greens, cucumber, fennel, coriander, mint, milk and ghee. Ease off chilli,
sour, salt, and the fermented (*Aṣṭāṅga Hṛdaya*, Sūtrasthāna 1). And never let
the fire go hungry: regular meals are themselves cooling, because an unfed fire
turns to anger.

**The day.** A calm, unhurried start, out of the midday sun; hard effort saved
for the cooler hours. A cool-water swish or oil-pulling, a tongue scraped. For
the fire, the cooling oils — sunflower, sandalwood. (Coconut is common in
modern practice; worth confirming with a practitioner.)

**The breath.** *Śītalī*, the cooling breath — drawing the air across the
tongue so it comes in cool, and letting it out soft. It eases heat and
sharpness.

**The body in motion.** Easy and non-competitive — a swim, gentle twists, a walk
in the cool of the day. The fire does best with ease over striving; it has
enough drive of its own.

**The season and the hour.** Pitta rises in summer, in the midday heat, and in
the hours around noon and midnight. Meet those times with coolness, shade, sweet
and bitter food, and nothing rushed.

## The classical voice

The texts return to one teaching: the fire that cooks our food is the same fire
that lights our days. Where it burns even, the body is bright — good colour,
steady strength, clear mind. Where it burns too hot, it consumes what it was
meant to sustain. *(After the Caraka Saṃhitā, Sūtrasthāna, on agni — rendered in
our own words.)*

## Today, try this

Take one thing off the fire today: skip the second coffee, step into the shade at
midday, or let one small irritation simply pass unanswered. Cool a single degree,
on purpose.

## In a breath

The fire is heat, sharpness, and intensity. Meet it with coolness, sweetness,
and calm — and feed it on time — and it warms the house instead of burning it.

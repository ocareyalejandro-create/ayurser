# The Three States of Mind (Sattva, Rajas, Tamas)

*Authored in our own voice, grounded in the classical texts (public domain),
informed by the teachers we credit in the References. We learned from them; we
did not copy them.*

---

## What they are

The body has its three forces; the mind has three of its own. Ayurveda calls them
the *mahā-guṇas* — the great qualities of mind — and where the body's forces shape
how we *feel*, these shape how we *are*: clear, or driven, or dull. They are
**sattva**, **rajas**, and **tamas**.

- **Sattva** — clarity, harmony, light. The mind at its best: calm, lucid,
  present, kind. Sattva is the quiet morning of the mind, when things are simply
  clear and you meet the day without grasping or dread.

- **Rajas** — movement, drive, fire. The energy that gets things done — ambition,
  passion, momentum — but restless with it: craving, agitation, the mind that
  cannot stop, the irritation when it is thwarted. Rajas builds; it rarely rests.

- **Tamas** — heaviness, inertia, dark. Stability and rest in their needed form —
  sleep, stillness, the ground — but in excess: dullness, fog, stuckness, the
  low heavy mood that will not move. Tamas holds; in excess it holds you down.

## We are all three

No one is only one of these. We move through all three in a single day — clear at
dawn (sattva), busy and driven by noon (rajas), heavy and spent by night (tamas)
— and across our lives. The aim is not to become *purely* sattvic; that is neither
possible nor human. The aim is gentler: to let **sattva lead more often** — to
widen the clear, calm ground, and to keep rajas and tamas in their proper, useful
measure rather than running the show.

## Growing the light

You do not force clarity. You make room for it, by how you live — and the moves
are the ones this whole book keeps returning to:

- **Toward sattva:** fresh, whole, simple food; time in nature; rhythm and enough
  rest; quiet, meditation, honesty, kindness; good company; less noise, less
  intoxicant, less endless input.
- **To cool rajas** (the wired, driven mind): calm and routine; slowing down; not
  feeding the fire with stimulation, argument, or hurry.
- **To lift tamas** (the heavy, foggy mind): movement, light, warmth, and
  stimulation — the same medicine as for the body's earth.

## Body and mind are kin

The two are not separate. A clear body lends a clear mind; good digestion and
*ojas* (the body's deep vitality) are close cousins of sattva, and heaviness or
depletion in the body pulls the mind down with it. This is why Ayurveda never
treats the mind apart from the life around it — the food, the sleep, the senses,
the rhythm all shape the weather of the mind.

And it names, exactly, the modern affliction: a life lived in *rajas* until it
burns down into *tamas* — wired and driven until, suddenly, flat and foggy and
unable to begin. The way back is not more drive. It is the slow, sattvic return:
warmth, rhythm, rest, simplicity, and the one quiet minute repeated until the
clarity comes back on its own.

## Today, try this

Do one *sattvic* thing on purpose today — ten quiet minutes in nature, a meal
eaten without a screen, a real conversation, a few slow breaths. Notice the mind
grow a shade clearer.

## In a breath

The mind has three weathers — clear (sattva), driven (rajas), heavy (tamas). We
hold all three; health is letting the clear one lead more of the time. Cool the
driven with calm, lift the heavy with movement, and feed clarity with simple
food, nature, rest, and kindness. And as ever: not *"you are tamasic,"* only
*"the mind is heavy today"* — weather, not a label.
